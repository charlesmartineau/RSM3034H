Why Variance Ratio > 1 Implies Momentum

The Variance Ratio Test: Compares the variance of q-period returns to q times the variance of 1-period returns. Under a random walk (no momentum), this ratio should equal 1.
VR(q) = Var(rt + rt-1 + ... + rt-q+1) / [q × Var(rt)]

Why VR > 1 Indicates Momentum:

Random Walk (VR ≈ 1): Returns are uncorrelated. When you sum 4 periods, variances simply add up: Var(4-period) = 4 × Var(1-period). This is because Var(X + Y) = Var(X) + Var(Y) when X and Y are uncorrelated.

Momentum (VR > 1): Returns are positively correlated. Positive returns tend to follow positive returns. When summing returns, you get: Var(X + Y) = Var(X) + Var(Y) + 2×Cov(X,Y). The positive covariance term makes the 4-period variance larger than expected, pushing VR above 1.

Intuition: With momentum, multi-period returns are more volatile than expected because trends persist. A positive shock doesn't just affect one period—it creates a string of positive returns, amplifying the cumulative volatility.

Mathematical Relationship:

For a variance ratio with lag q:

VR(q) ≈ 1 + 2 × Σ(1 - k/q) × ρk

where ρk is the autocorrelation at lag k.

When autocorrelations are positive (momentum), VR > 1
When autocorrelations are negative (mean reversion), VR < 1
